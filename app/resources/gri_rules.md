# General Rules of Interpretation (GRI)

The GRI are the legal framework for classifying goods in the UK Tariff. They must be applied
in numerical order — GRI 1 takes precedence, then GRI 2 only if GRI 1 doesn't resolve it, and
so on. Most goods are classified by GRI 1 alone.

---

## GRI 1 — Heading text and legal notes first

Classification is determined by the terms of the headings and any relative section or chapter
notes. The text of the heading itself, plus any exclusion or inclusion notes, is the primary
classification tool.

**In plain English:** Read the heading. If the heading description matches your product and
there are no notes that exclude it, you're done.

**Example:**
Heading 0808 says "Apples, pears and quinces, fresh". A fresh apple classifies here under
GRI 1 — no further rules needed.

**Common GRI 1 traps:**
- Chapter/Section notes can *exclude* things that sound like they should be there
  (e.g., Chapter 63 Note 1 excludes surgical dressings even though they're textile-based)
- "Not elsewhere specified or included" (NES/NEC/NEC) headings are catch-alls — only use
  them if no specific heading fits

---

## GRI 2 — Incomplete articles and mixtures

**GRI 2(a):** An incomplete or unfinished article classifies as the complete article, provided
it has the essential character of the finished article.

**Example:** An unassembled bicycle frame with wheels — classifies as a bicycle, not as
parts, because it has the essential character of a bicycle.

**GRI 2(b):** A material or substance classifies with the pure form of that material/substance.
A mixture of two materials may be classifiable under two headings — proceed to GRI 3.

---

## GRI 3 — Goods classifiable under two or more headings

Apply only when GRI 1 and 2 leave ambiguity. Use in this order:

**GRI 3(a) — Most specific description wins**
The heading with the most specific description of the goods takes priority over a more general
one. A heading that names a product is more specific than one that names a class.

**Example:** Printer ink cartridges — Chapter 84 (computer peripherals) vs. Chapter 32 (inks).
Chapter 84 specifically describes printer cartridges, so it wins.

**GRI 3(b) — Essential character**
When goods are mixtures, composite articles, or sets put up for retail sale, classify by the
component that gives the product its essential character.

Essential character is often determined by:
- The role of each component in relation to the use of the article
- Value, weight, bulk, or quantity (as a secondary guide)

**Example:** A gift set containing a bottle of perfume and a small cosmetics bag. If the perfume
is clearly the principal item (higher value, main selling point), classify as perfume (Ch 33).

**GRI 3(c) — Last heading in numerical order**
If essential character still cannot be determined, classify under whichever heading comes last
in numerical order. This is a last resort.

---

## GRI 4 — Most akin goods

If no heading covers the goods even remotely, classify under the heading for the goods most
similar to them.

This rule is very rarely needed in practice. If you reach GRI 4, reconsider whether you have
the right chapter.

---

## GRI 5 — Cases and containers

**GRI 5(a):** Camera cases, instrument cases, gun cases, drawing instrument cases, necklace
cases and similar containers, which are suitable for long-term use, are presented with the
articles for which they are intended, and are of a kind normally sold with such articles,
classify with those articles.

**GRI 5(b):** Packing materials and packing containers presented with the goods they enclose
classify with those goods, unless they are clearly suitable for repetitive use (e.g. a metal
drum).

**In plain English:** A camera in its camera bag classifies as a camera. The bag goes with it.
But a bulk steel container you ship goods in does not get reclassified as your goods.

---

## GRI 6 — Subheading level classification

The rules above apply at the heading level (4 digits). When drilling down to the subheading
level (6, 8, or 10 digits), the same rules apply again but only comparing subheadings within
the same heading.

**In plain English:** Once you've picked the 4-digit heading under GRI 1-5, use GRI 1-5 again
to pick the right 6-digit subheading within it, then again for 8-digit, then 10-digit.

Subheading notes (found at the start of some chapters) only apply at the subheading level and
cannot affect heading selection.

---

## Practical classification checklist

Use this order every time:

1. What is this product? (its nature, not its use)
2. Are there any chapter or section notes that include or exclude it? (GRI 1)
3. Does one heading specifically describe it? (GRI 1)
4. Is it incomplete/unfinished? (GRI 2a)
5. Is it a mixture or composite that falls under two headings? (GRI 2b → GRI 3)
6. Apply 3a (most specific), then 3b (essential character), then 3c (last in order)
7. Drill down through subheadings using GRI 6

---

## Binding Tariff Information (BTI)

When classification is genuinely uncertain — especially for novel products, composite goods,
or high-value regular shipments — the trader should apply for a Binding Tariff Information
ruling from HMRC. This is:
- **Free** to apply for
- **Legally binding** on HMRC for 3 years
- **Takes up to 120 days**
- Applied for through the UK Trade Tariff portal

A BTI removes classification risk entirely. For a business importing the same product regularly,
it is strongly recommended.
